# Conversion Copywriting Skill

Write website copy that drives action, not just describes services. Every line must earn its place.

## When to use
Use this skill when generating any text content for a client website — headlines, subheadlines, CTAs, section copy, testimonial framing, and meta descriptions.

## Core Framework: PAS (Problem → Agitate → Solution)

### Every page hero follows this structure:

**Problem** — Name the specific pain the target audience has. Be concrete.
Bad: "Life is stressful."
Good: "Your neck hasn't turned properly in months."

**Agitate** — Make them feel the cost of inaction. One or two sentences.
Bad: "This can get worse over time."
Good: "That stiffness becomes headaches. The headaches become missed deadlines. Your GP says 'try stretching' and hands you a leaflet."

**Solution** — Position the client's service as the specific answer with a tangible benefit.
Bad: "We offer professional massage therapy."
Good: "One 45-minute deep tissue session and you'll turn your head both ways before you reach the car park."

## CTA Language Rules

ALWAYS first-person, benefit-focused:
- "Book my free session" — not "Book now"
- "Get my free quote" — not "Request a quote"
- "See my pricing options" — not "View pricing"
- "Start my free trial" — not "Sign up"
- "Send my message" — not "Submit"

NEVER use:
- "Learn more" (vague, no commitment)
- "Click here" (meaningless)
- "Submit" (bureaucratic)
- "Get started" (with what?)

## Headline Rules

1. **Specificity over cleverness.** "We've soundproofed 300+ Manchester flats since 2018" beats "Your peace and quiet, delivered."
2. **Numbers build trust.** Use them whenever possible: years, clients served, rating, response time.
3. **Name the location.** Local businesses rank for "[service] + [location]". Put it in the headline or subheadline.
4. **No jargon the customer wouldn't use.** They say "fix my boiler", not "heating system remediation services."
5. **Maximum 12 words for a headline.** Say it shorter.

## Section Copy Rules

- Lead with the benefit, then explain the feature.
  Bad: "Our saunas use imported Finnish birch wood." (feature)
  Good: "The heat feels different when the wood is right. We import Finnish birch for the authentic löyly experience." (benefit → feature)

- One idea per paragraph. Three lines maximum.

- End every section with momentum toward the CTA. The last sentence should make the reader want to act, not just nod.

## Testimonial Framing

Don't just dump quotes. Frame them:
- Add context: "After 6 months of back pain..."
- Highlight the transformation: what was life like before vs. after
- Use the customer's real words — imperfect grammar is more believable
- Include name, role/context, and ideally a photo or location

## Meta Descriptions

- 150-160 characters
- Include primary keyword + location
- Include a benefit and a soft CTA
- Format: "[Benefit statement]. [Service] in [Location]. [CTA]."
- Example: "Authentic Finnish sauna sessions in central Manchester. Private hire and corporate wellness packages. Book your first session free."

## Words & Phrases to NEVER Use

These are AI-slop signals that destroy credibility:
- "Elevate" / "Transform" / "Revolutionize" / "Unleash"
- "Cutting-edge" / "State-of-the-art" / "Next-generation"
- "Seamless" / "Seamlessly" / "Effortless" / "Effortlessly"
- "Unlock the power of" / "Harness the potential"
- "Your journey starts here" / "Welcome to [Business]"
- "We are passionate about" / "We are committed to"
- "One-stop shop" / "Go-to destination"
- "In today's fast-paced world"
- "Look no further"
- "At [Business], we believe..."

## Tone Calibration

Read the CLIENT_BRIEF.md tone selections and calibrate:
- **Warm & inviting**: conversational, second-person ("you"), contractions ("you'll"), short sentences
- **Premium & refined**: measured pace, fewer contractions, precise word choice, confident understatement
- **Bold & energetic**: punchy, short paragraphs, imperative verbs, exclamation used sparingly
- **Technical & authoritative**: data-led, specific credentials, third-person where appropriate
- **Playful & casual**: colloquial, humor where natural, informal structure

Most local businesses should sound like a knowledgeable friend recommending something good — not like a corporate brochure and not like a used car salesman.
